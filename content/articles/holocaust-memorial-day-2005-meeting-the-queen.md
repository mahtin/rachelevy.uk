To mark Holocaust Memorial Day 2005, I was invited to a reception at St. James’s Palace and was formally presented to Her Majesty The Queen.
(St James's Palace is just half a mile away from Buckingham Palace.)
I was among 600 Survivors and Liberators who were honoured by the Queen.

![Holocaust Memorial Day 2005 Meeting the Queen](/images/holocaust-memorial-day-2005-meeting-the-queen-2.jpg){: .right-small }
January 27 is the UK's Holocaust Memorial Day and in 2005 it marked the 60th Anniversary of the Liberation of Auschwitz Concentration Camp.

Back in London, the Catford and Bromley Synagogue newsletter (written by Joan Goldberg) soon afwards wrote:

>	Before meeting the Queen, Rachel was informed of the protocol of the day.
>	No spouses, no hats, no speaking until spoken to, and not to offer your hand until the Queen offers hers.
>	There were to be 600 people at St James Palace, all divided into small groups and into specific rooms in the Palace.
>	Rachel was to be among the first group to be introduced to the Queen, in the Throne room.
>	The whole day was beautifully orchestrated. When the Queen and Prince Phillip entered, they proceded down the length of the room, one on each side, meeting the Survivors.
>
>	When the Queen reached Rachel, she asked her how old Rachel was when she was in the Camps, and which Camps she was in, at what age she was liberated, and when she reached Britain.
>	The Queen was sympathetic and listened intently to Rachel.
>
>	Rachel's summing up of the day is that it was a wonderful honour to be invited to St. James' and having a conversation with the Queen was certainly one of the highlights of her life.
>	Rachel's family in Israel were on the phone before Rachel managed to get home.
>	They were very excited as they had seen the whole conversation on their TV, and had recorded it for posterity.
>
>	Following their audience with the Queen, the Survivors were driven to Westminster Hall, where the Service of Thanksgiving was televised.
>	The service at Westminster Hall was an acknowledgement of the Holocaust.
>	Accolades and achievements were not shown.
>	The Survivors are all aware of what they have achieved and that it is a miracle that they have remained sane and well adjusted.
>
>	"*Well,*" says Rachel "*not completely sane. After all, we are only human*".
>	I am sure all our community will join me in wishing Rachel, all her family, and all her fellow Survivors, "*col hacavod*" (well done)
> 	on her meeting the Queen, but especially on "staying sane" and maybe it was the Queen who was honoured.
