In May 2013, at the age of 83, I returned for the first time to my birthplace of **Bhutz**, a small Jewish village in former Czechoslovakia's
Carpathian Mountains from which I had been deported by the Nazis in 1944 at age fourteen.

The visit followed nearly a decade of research by my family to locate the village, whose name and administrative identity had shifted across Czech,
Hungarian, Romanian, and now Ukrainian jurisdictions during the twentieth century.

I travelled with a multigenerational group of seventeen relatives representing three continents, including my children, grandchildren, nieces,
and cousins from the directly related Levy, Slomovic, and Steinmetz families.

Upon reaching the area—now identified as Velykyi Bychkiv/Byouts and Malyi Bychkiv/Byouts, I met local residents who remembered the pre‑war Jewish
community and my family’s flour mill.
One 98‑year‑old Romanian speaking villager recalled my grandfather, Dudye Slomovic, and described how local families had attempted to hide
Jewish residents in the surrounding forests during the first Nazi roundup.

Members of my family also visited the site of the former Jewish cemetery, destroyed during the communist period, where they lit a Yahrzeit
candle and recited Kaddish.

Our group continued to Solotvyno, the location of the wartime ghetto from which my family had been deported, and held a further memorial
at the commemorative plaque marking the site.

The journey marked my first and so-far only return to my childhood home and served as a significant moment of remembrance for both my family
and surviving local witnesses to the village’s destroyed Jewish community.
