**Mark Curtis** is not an actor in the play. He is not the director. He is not the writer.
And yet, without him, *Rachel* - the play, would never have reached the thousands of students who have now seen it.

Mark became the play's champion, its advocate, and in many ways its guardian.

He understood immediately that the play was not just a piece of theatre, it was an educational tool, a bridge between history and the present day.
He recognised that young people today are bombarded with misinformation, conspiracy theories, and online hatred.
He knew that my story, told truthfully and creatively, could cut through that noise.

So he took it upon himself to make sure the play was seen.

Mark Curtis was the quiet force behind the scenes.

## Rewind
My story, of a childhood in the tiny village of [Bhutz](/blog/bhutz), of deportation, of surviving Auschwitz and Bergen-Belsen,
and of rebuilding a life in [Millisle Northern Ireland](/blog/millisle/) and then London,
has always been something I shared to help others understand the consequences of hatred and the importance of compassion.

## How the play was born

The play *Rachel* was created in association with the theatre companies [Small Nose](smallnose.co.uk) and [Trestle Theatre Company](trestle.org.uk).
Both companies are known for physical theatre, mask work, and powerful storytelling.
When they approached me, they didn't want to sensationalise my experiences.  They wanted to honour them.

They listened, really listened, to my memories. They studied the history. They worked with sensitivity, humility, and a deep sense of responsibility.
The result was a production that captured not only the horror of what happened, but also the resilience, humour, and hope that helped me survive.

But a play is different. A play breathes. It moves. It reaches people in a way that spoken testimony alone sometimes cannot.
And the reason *Rachel* has reached so many schools, so many young audiences, is because of one person whose dedication has astonished me: Mark Curtis.

## Taking *Rachel* into schools

![Rachel - Trestle Theatre Company](/images/rachel-trestle-theatre-co.jpg){: .right-small }
But even the most meaningful play needs someone to carry it into the world. That someone was Mark.
Mark worked tirelessly to bring the play into schools across the UK.
He contacted teachers, headteachers, Holocaust education organisations, and local councils.
He arranged tours, scheduled performances, and handled the endless logistics that come with moving a theatre production from one school hall to another.

He didn't do this for recognition. He did it because he believed, truly believed, that young people needed to hear my story.

And the impact has been extraordinary.

Students who had never heard of Bhutz, or the Kindertransport, or the liberation of Bergen-Belsen, suddenly found themselves immersed in a living history lesson.
Teachers wrote to me saying their students were moved to tears, asking thoughtful questions, and wanting to learn more.
Some said it changed the way their pupils understood prejudice and kindness.

That is Mark's doing.

## Why this matters today

When I first agreed to let my life become a stage play, I never imagined how far it would travel, or how deeply it would resonate with young people.

I have spent decades speaking in schools, community centres, synagogues, and universities.
I have shared my story in documentaries, including the BBC's Confronting Holocaust Denial with [David Baddiel](/blog/confronting-holocaust-denial-with-david-baddiel/).
I have spoken because I believe that silence is dangerous.

But as I grow older, I cannot be everywhere. I cannot speak in every school.
The play *Rachel* allows my story to continue travelling even when I cannot.
And Mark ensures that it does.

In a time when antisemitism is rising, when historical truth is questioned, and when survivors are becoming fewer, people like Mark are essential.
He is part of the chain of memory, one that must never be broken.

## My gratitude

I often say that survival is not something I achieved alone. I survived because of the kindness of others-strangers, friends, and later, my family.
Today, I see Mark as part of that same thread of kindness.
He has taken my story and carried it forward with care, respect, and determination.
He has helped ensure that the next generation learns not only what happened, but why it matters.

For that, I am deeply grateful.
