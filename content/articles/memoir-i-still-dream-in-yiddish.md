In 2025, I published my memoir, **I Still Dream In Yiddish**, as part of the UK-based **My Voice** project,
a national initiative to preserve and share the life stories of Holocaust survivors living in the United Kingdom.

The book recounts my early life in Bhutz, my deportation to Auschwitz and Bergen-Belsen, and my postwar journey through Millisle and London.
It also explores my reflections on memory, identity, and the enduring emotional legacy of the Holocaust.
The title refers to the language of my childhood and the dreams that still connect me to my lost family and community.

The memoir was developed through a series of recorded interviews and writing workshops facilitated by the My Voice team and editorial guidance from project volunteers.
I worked closely with editors to shape her narrative, ensuring that my voice and memories remained central to the final publication.

The book was launched at a public event in Hampstead, London, on 14 July 2025, alongside memoirs by fellow survivors Kurt Marx and Jacques Weisser.
The launch was attended by my family members, educators, and representatives from Yad Vashem UK and The Fed, who praised the project as *a victory over the silence*.

[**I Still Dream In Yiddish**](https://myvoice.org.uk/book/i-still-dream-in-yiddish/) is now part of the growing [My Voice](https://myvoice.org.uk) Collection,
which aims to preserve the testimonies of Holocaust survivors for future generations and support Holocaust education across the UK.

The book is now available in paperback from [Amazon](https://www.amazon.com/My-Voice-Remarkable-Holocaust-Survivors/dp/180707319X) and other suppliers.

An excerpt from the book:

>	**Liberation by the kind British**
>
>	Bergen-Belsen was liberated on 15 April 1945, shortly after my aunt died.
>	In the days before liberation, there were fewer and fewer Nazis in the camp.
>	Suddenly, there was no leftover food in the bins as even the Germans had no food anymore.
>	We heard planes overhead, but we didn't know freedom was coming until the trucks filled with soldiers drove in.
>	We knew they weren't Nazis because the Nazis had already fled.
>	I thought they were Americans but the soldiers were British.
>	They started talking to us in English but how could I know what they were saying?
>	I didn't understand a word.
>	All we wanted was *essen*, food.
>
>	In their kindness, they threw us tins of corned beef from their rations.
>	The soldiers meant well, but these fatty foods weren't good for our stomachs and many of us became even more ill, with diarrhoea and tummy trouble.
>	It took a day or so before they brought in drums of clean water and more medical help.
>	A few days after the British arrived, we were taken to a former army camp nearby where there were big buildings with accommodation.
>	We must have been taken in trucks because I couldn't walk.
>	This became known as a Displaced Persons (DP) camp.
>	Once it was empty, the actual camp at Bergen-Belsen was burned down to the ground to stop the spread of disease.
>
>	In the DP camp, there were doctors and nurses who all spoke English.
>	I don't think the doctors knew where to start, but the nurses were fantastic.
>	They sat there with combs, clearing our heads and bodies of lice and cutting off hair if they had to.
>	They must have got lice on themselves in the process because they were crawling everywhere.
>	There was a language barrier and we didn't know what they were saying, but they were very kind and caring and had lots of patience.
>	They cleaned and nursed us and gave us medication to clear up the diarrhoea.
>	They found us clothes, cooked for us and we slept in clean beds.
>	In time, we grew healthier and stronger.
