*Written by Shelley Levy October 2010 -*

Rachel Levy was born Ruzena Slomovic in 1930 in a Shetel (Jewish Village)
called Bhutz in the Carpathian mountains. In these times the Shetels had
specific roles. One would make bread, another leather, another cloths, etc.
Bhutz was the religious Shetel where nearly all the Jewish men devoted their
lives to learning Torah and Talmud, many becoming Rabbis. They were (and
are) called Yeshiva Buchas.

In these religious homes the mothers were expected to make a living to
support their husband and family. Again this is the case today, with the
extended family (especially grandparents) supporting a religious family.

![Slomovic Family Photo (1939)](/images/slomovic-family-photo-1939.jpg)

Rachel's Mum Shlima owned a shop (which was the front room of their home)
and sold merchandise that included cloths and food (basic essentials). They
also grew their own food and kept chickens (as did most of the residents of
Bhutz).

Rachel had one older brother Chaskal, two younger sisters Rivka and Eta and
a younger brother Ben Zvi. As the oldest daughter Rachel was expected to
help raise the family, cook, clean, and help in the shop.

The shop not only served the local residents of Bhutz, (mostly Jewish) but
also the 'mountain people' who would come down to buy supplies and sell
skins of animals they had caught.

Chaskal was being trained to enter Yeshiva (a religious school), and Rachel
was being trained to become a good Jewish wife and mother like every
Jewish girl in Bhutz.

In 1942 the Hungarians came and took Rachel's Dad away (Solomon). He
was never heard of again. We do not know what happened to him, where he
was murdered, or by whom. But it is possible he is in a mass grave in the
woods somewhere?

In 1944 the Hungarians came back to take all the Jewish people away. They
had come before and all the Jewish people had gone and hid in the
mountains. But this time they were caught.

They were taken to an initial holding Ghetto in Solotnavo (Slatina) and then
deported in cattle trucks to Auschwitz. They were packed in too densely in the
cattle trucks for them all to survive the journey.

None of them knew about the death camps.

On debarkation at Auschwitz they were met by many soldiers with very
vicious dogs.

Rachel’s Mum and 3 younger siblings were sent to the left. I.e. they were sent
immediately to the gas chambers and not even recorded (by the officious
Germans).

Rachel and Chaskal were sent to the right (i.e. not murdered immediately).

There are many very unpleasant memories of Rachel in Auschwitz. These
can be found in her testimonials and other peoples’ testimonials. One of
Rachel’s worst memories was the liquidation of the Gypsy camp (i.e.
murdering of all the Gypsies). This was a small camp in Auschwitz, and the
Gypsies were allowed more privileges than the Jews, but the screams from
that camp still haunt her.

When the Russian army got close to Auschwitz all the prisoners that could
walk were taken on 'death marches' to Bergen-Belesn. Many people died on
the marches. Rachel remembers staying at a concentration camp
Birenboumel on the march. There is very little information on this camp, so
she was lucky to get out of this one alive! Again the German soldiers were
brutal on this march, killing women and children for fun.

Somehow she survived the ‘death march’ and arrived at Bergen-Belsen. Her
memories of Bergen-Belsen are even worse than Auschwitz. There was no
food. But she found an aunt at Bergen-Belsen who was very ill. Her aunt had
typhoid which was rife in the camp. She remembers her aunt very ill next to
her asking for water. There was no water so she crawled out to a puddle in
the mud (she was too ill to walk also having typhoid) and carried dirty water in
her mouth for her aunt. Unfortunately her aunt died before Bergen-Belsen was
liberated by the British. However before her aunt died she asked two of her
friends (also in Bergen-Belsen) to look after look after Rachel (knowing she
was dying).

When the British liberated Bergen-Belsen they were so sorry for the victims in
the camp that they gave their rations to the inmates. As most of the inmates
had not eaten proper food for years this killed several inmates, and made the
others even sicker. The German camp guards were retained by the British to
run the camp. They just stopped murdering the inmates.

After being nursed back to health (Unfortunately thousands died) the two
friends of her aunt wanted to go to Palestine. They thought the journey was
too dangerous for Rachel and didn’t want to be burdened with the
responsibility of taking Rachel, so left Rachel with an Uncle of theirs who had
a farm. Fortunately the very same day her brother Chaskal who had also
survived the holocaust (somehow) turned up and took Rachel back to the
displaced persons centre in Prague.

Chaskal had been back to their home town of Bhutz, and said that Christians
had seized their home and she should not go back. There were no Jews left in
Bhutz.

At the same time the British Jews (‘Central British Fund for German Jewry’ -
CBF – now known as ‘World Jewish Relief’ WJR) learnt about the Holocaust
and raised enough money to pay the British government for 1000 Jewish
children orphan refugees (in those days refugees had to pay to enter the UK!).
Only 732 Jewish orphan children from the Holocaust could be found and were
invited to the UK. Rachel and Chaskal were amongst them. This group is
known as “The Boys” as mostly boys survived. These children had no
knowledge of English, spoke several different languages came from all over
Europe, had little or no education, and had forgot basic things like how to use
a knife and fork. The children were sent to various hostels organised (and
paid for) by the CBF. There they learnt English, and a profession. Rachel was
taught sewing to become a dress maker.

![Chaskel Slomovic (1946)](/images/chaskel-slomovic-1946.jpg){: .right-small }
Her brother Chaskal was diagnosed with TB and sent to a sanitarium in
Essex. So Rachel moved to a hostel in Herne Hill so she could be near and
visit her brother. It took years for Chaskal to recover, but he used the time to
do correspondence O and A levels.

At a Jewish youth group Rachel became friendly with a local girl Rebecca
Mendoza, and Rebecca persuaded her parents to let Rachel come and live
with them (which both Rachel and Rebecca’s parents were very happy with).

Chaskal recovered from TB, and became a successful business man. He
married one of his nurses and they had two girls Fiona and Sarah. The
Holocaust had made Chaskal loose his faith. This is not the case with Rachel.
Unfortunately Chaskal died very young (at 42) from heart and lung failure. He
was treated at Harefield hospital by Sir Magdi Yacoub in the 1970’s and was
one of the first patients to receive a heart and lung transplant. The operation
was a success, but he died of rejection complications later.

At another Jewish youth group Rachel met Phineas Levy a local Herne Hill
boy from Brixton Synagogue (Brixton used to be a Jewish Area), they fell in
love and married. They have 2 children Martin and Shelley.

Rachel is still very active and travels the planet to see her family (America,
Israel, South Africa, etc.) and friends. She is now considering going to the
Ukraine to find the village Bhutz where she was born.

Rachel was an ordinary girl born into a religious home but placed into extreme
and extraordinary situations by anti-Semitism and the Holocaust.

This has made her an extraordinary person.

There are many other parts and incidents in Rachel’s life that are not
documented here. However with this outline, I am hoping that a documentary
can be made of her journey to the Ukraine to try and find the village of Bhutz
where she was born and is now almost certainly the sole Jewish survivor.

*- Written by Shelley Levy October 2010*
